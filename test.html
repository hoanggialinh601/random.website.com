1. Eukaryotic Cell Ultrastructure

i. Cell Theory

+) The cell is the basic unit of life. It is the building block of each organism that carries out all the essential processes of life that makes the organism a living entity.

+) All organisms are made up of cells. Organisms composed of a single cell are unicellular whereas those made up of many cells are multicellular.

+) All cells arise from pre-existing cells by division.

+) The cell contains information (hereditary materials/DNA) which is used as instructions for growth, development and functioning.

+) The cell is the functional unit of life in which chemical reactions that maintain life take place within it.

ii. Cellular organisation

+) Eukaryotes (which means “true nucleus”): inclusive of all plants and animals, the cell of a eukaryote possesses a true nucleus containing genetic materials known as DNA, enclosed within the nuclear envelope.

+) Prokaryotes (which means “before the nucleus”): inclusive of bacteria and archaea, the cell of a prokaryote lacks a true nucleus. The genetic material is not enclosed within membranes but lies freely within the cytoplasm.

iii. Eukaryotic cells

+) Has at least 3 major structural features that make up its protoplasm (living matter of the cell):

  • A cell surface membrane that forms a boundary between the internal and external environment of the cell. It is partially permeable and controls substances entering and leaving the cell.
  • A nucleus that contains genetic materials (DNA) and directs cellular activities.
  • The cytoplasm which contains the following:
  • Cytosol: a fluid component/aqueous solution of various essential ions, soluble organic compounds such as sugars and amino acids, and soluble proteins, many of which are enzymes.
  • Organelles: subcellular components which carry out specific functions to maintain the cell or a multicellular organism. May or may not be membrane bound.
  • Cytoskeleton: a network of fine strands of globular proteins. These support the cell and maintain its overall shape.
  • In addition, plant cells have a rigid cell wall external to the cell surface membrane.

iv. Membrane systems and organelles within cytoplasm

+) Compartmentalisation: the cytoplasm is organised into compartments called organelles. Organelles may or may not have a membrane. Each organelle has its own function for the cell or the organism. Specific chemical reactions are located within each organelle.

Nucleus

Largest organelle (10-20 μm in diameter), spherical or ovoid in shape.

Surrounded by a double membrane known as nuclear envelope:

  • Outer membrane of the nuclear envelope is continuous with another organelle called the endoplasmic reticulum
  • Inner membrane is in contact with the contents of the nucleus
  • Is perforated with pores known as nuclear pores

    Nearly all the cell’s DNA (organised into chromosomes) is found here.

    One or more nucleolus (plural: nucleoli) within the nucleoplasm.

 Control of cellular activities by regulating protein and enzyme synthesis.

Nuclear division is the basis of cell replication.

Nucleolus is responsible for synthesis and partial assembly of ribosomes.

Cell surface membrane

Basic structure: a phospholipid bilayer with the hydrophobic tails on the interior of the membrane and the hydrophilic heads on the exterior.

Each phospholipid is made up of a glycerol molecule attached to a phosphate group and 2 fatty acid chains.

Additional components:

  • Cholesterol
  • Glycoproteins/ glycolipids
  • Transport proteins
 Partially permeable membrane

Acts as a barrier between the cytoplasm and the environment

Prevents most water-soluble substances from entering or leaving the cell

Centrioles (in animal cells only)

Cylindrical (tube-like) structures composed of microtubules: a single centriole consists of 9 microtubule triplets arranged in the shape of a cylinder.

2 centrioles make up a centrosome.

Found:

  • In pairs only in animal cells, positioned in the cytoplasm outside the nucleus but close to it
  • As single structures at the base of cilia and flagella
 Produce spindle fibres which attach to chromosomes and separate them during cell division

Involved in the formation of cilia and flagella

Ribosomes

About 20 nm in diameter

Has no membranes

Made up of protein and ribonucleic acid (ribosomal RNA)

Each ribosome comprises 2 sub-units - a large subunit and a small subunit.

Location:

  • Attached to the rER

    or

  • As free ribosomes in the cytosol
 Site of protein synthesis

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

Originates from the outer membrane of the nuclear envelope

Consists of a network of membranous tubes or sacs called cisternae (singular: cisterna)

2 types of ER:

  • Rough ER (rER): ribosomes are present on its surface
  • Smooth ER (sER): lacks ribosomes, more tubular
 sER: synthesises lipids, steroid hormones (detoxification)

rER: synthesises and transports proteins

Golgi apparatus

Consists of a stack of flattened membrane-bound sacs also known as cisternae

Continually being formed at cis face (receiving side) by fusion of a vesicle from the ER and continually budded off at the trans face (shipping side) where vesicles are pinched off

Chemically modifies, sorts and transports molecules within it for secretion across the cell membrane or for delivery to other parts of the cell

Lysosome formation

Lysosomes

Small spherical vesicles, 0.2 - 0.5 μm in diameter, or larger in plants

Bound by a single membrane

Formed from a the Golgi apparatus

Contains a concentrated mixture of hydrolytic digestive enzymes

Digestion of materials taken in by cells (in endocytosis and phagocytosis)

Autophagy: digestion of worn-out/ improperly functioning organelles

Autolysis: self-digestion of a cell by the release of lysosome contents within the cell

Mitochondrion (plural: mitochondria)

Rod-shaped or cylindrical-shaped organelle with size varying within the range 0.5 - 1.5 μm wide and 3.0 - 10.0 μm long.

Bound by a double membrane, an outer and inner membrane separated by the inner-membrane space.

The outer membrane is a smooth and continuous boundary. The inner membrane is extensively folded to form cristae (singular: crista) that project into the interior (the matrix).

Within the matrix, hereditary materials (circular DNA, RNA) and ribosomes can be found.

Involved in cellular respiration to release energy

Chloroplast (in plant cells only)

Are large organelles, typically a few μm in diameter and 5 -10 μm long. Apart from the large central vacuole and nucleus, they are larger than any other structures within the plant cell.

Bound by a double membrane:

  • Outer membrane is smooth and continuous.
  • Inner membrane gives rise to membranes called lamellae (singular: lamella) or thylakoids which extends throughout the interior.

    Interior of the chloroplast is gel-like matrix called stroma.

    Within the stroma: thylakoids are stacked into grana (singular: granum), which are joined together by the intergranal lamellae.

    Contains photosynthetic pigments (e.g. chlorophyll) which make the chloroplasts appear green.

 Site of photosynthesis

Vacuole

Fluid filled structures bound a single membrane.

In animal cells: usually relatively small and less permanent (called vesicles)

In plant cells, a large central, permanent vacuole surrounded by a membrane (tonoplast), containing cell sap.

Aids in entry of water, an important process in cell expansion during cell growth, as well as in the normal water relations of plants.

Contains pigments in solution (e.g. anthocyanins)

Hydrolytic enzymes may sometimes be present, making the vacuole act as a lysosome

Waste products may accumulate within the vacuole

Some of the dissolved substances act as food reserves

Cell wall (in plant cells only)

Surrounds plant cells, external to the cell surface membrane

Consists predominantly of cellulose

Cell wall materials are strong yet permeable therefore allowing the free passage of substances into and out of the cell.

Mechanical support and protective layer

v. Protein synthesis

+) Genetic information from the nucleus directs the synthesis of particular proteins using ribosomes.

+) For proteins that are to be secreted out of the cell (extracellular protein) → protein secretory pathway:

  1. Ribosomes bound to the rough endoplasmic reticulum (rER) synthesise the polypeptide chain into the rER.
  2. The protein becomes enclosed in an ER/ transport vesicle. The ER vesicle containing the protein buds off from the ER.
  3. The ER vesicle travels and fuses with the cis-face of the Golgi apparatus.
  4. The Golgi apparatus chemically modifies, sorts and transports the proteins. The proteins move through the Golgi apparatus by Golgi vesicles budding off from one cisterna and fusing with another.
  5. The secretory vesicle containing the protein buds off from the trans-face of the Golgi apparatus, travels and fuses with the cell surface membrane, thus releasing the protein out of the cell.

+) For proteins that are needed for use within the cell → synthesised by free ribosomes.

vi. Plant cells and animal cells

+) Similarities:

  • Present in both cells is the protoplasm - cell surface membrane, nucleus, and cytoplasm consisting of organelles, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, mitochondria and ribosomes.
  • Chromosomes are composed of linear DNA wound around proteins

+) Differences:

Structural feature Plant cell Animal cell
Cell wall Present, contains cellulose Absent
Lysosomes Usually absent Often present
Chloroplasts Usually present Absent
Vacuoles Present, large and central Small and scattered or absent
Centrioles Usually absent Present

+) Care of the microscope:

  • Always use both hands when carrying: one hand on the arm and one hand under the base for support. Carry it close to the body and always carry in an upright position.
  • Do not tilt the microscope forward as the eyepiece might fall off
  • When placing the microscope on a table, keep it away from the edge.
  • Do not touch the lens surface with finger as the small amount of oil on the surface of the skin will leave a film on the glass.
  • When unpacking the microscope, make sure:
  • The 4X objective lens is aligned to the round glass of the stage
  • The stage is at the highest position
  • After all the steps above, uncoil the electrical wires.
  • Never clean exposed eyepiece, objective lens or condenser lens with anything other than lens paper as the magnifying lens are easily scratched.
  • Wipe off any moisture that may get on or into the stage or moving parts of the microscope.
  • When packing the microscope, make sure to:
  • Adjust the 4X objective lens is aligned to the round glass of the stage
  • Adjust the stage is at the highest position
  • Coil the electrical wires
  • Finally, lower the stage to lowest point.

+) Step by step guide on using a microscope:

  1. Plug in the microscope - excess wire must not dangle over the edge of the table. Switch on the microscope (switch is at the bottom right).
  2. Position 4X objective lends to be facing the stage opening/ hole. Turn nosepiece to move objective lens in place. (Must start with 4X objective lens before moving on.)
  3. Place the slide on the microscope stage with the specimen directly over the stage opening/ hole. Clip the slide in place and move the slide accordingly using the stage control knob.
  4. Focus using the coarse adjustment knob. Turn the knob to raise the stage to the highest position. Position eye at the eyepiece, and while looking into the eyepiece, slowly lower stage with coarse adjustment knob. The specimen should appear in the field of view and a sharp image is obtained.

    Note: field of view is the diameter of the circle of light that can be seen when looking through the eyepiece.

    Adjust the distance between the 2 eyepieces so both eyes can see one complete field of view at the same time. Slowly move on to the 10X, 40X, 60X objective lens and adjust accordingly.
