R&U - Comprehension 🤓

ⓘ When attempting R&U questions, it is advised that:

• Read the title of the text and the source at the back. This prepares your brain for the passage. Afterwards, annotate and make word clouds about possible related points (What, Where, When, Why, Who, How).

• Summarise each paragraph in the passage with succinct words and phrases.

• Ask and answer relevant questions related to the paragraphs in the passage:

◦ What are the points that the writer is making? What is the writer's central argument?

◦ Why do people support this viewpoint? Why are people against this viewpoint?

R&U questions consist of several types, including but not limited to:

• Vocabulary questions

• Explanation questions

• Inference questions

• Summary questions

Some R&U questions belong to several categories.


📖 Vocabulary questions

To approach vocabulary questions, identify the word form and verb form (if possible). The answer should reflect the tense of the verb if a verb is present. Contextualise all words to the passage. The question would usually have the word "mean" or "meaning".

💡 Is the question asking something positive or negative? Focus on the connotations of both the question and the passage.

E.g. 1. “In the past 20 years, Singapore has seen a very different wave of immigration compared to the movement of the pre-war years. Our total population has grown by almost a fifth, and much of this is due to the higher immigration and more foreigners becoming permanent residents to augment ultra-low fertility rates.”

Explain the meaning of the word "augment". [1]

⇒ Definition: “augment” means to increase, to add in.


E.g. 2. “One aspect that might be overlooked however is that many immigrants are presumed to fit within the Chinese and Indian labels, being of East Asian or South Asian ancestry.”

What does the word “fit” mean in this context? [1]

⇒ Definition: “augment” means to belong.


E.g. 3. “In more recent history, our combined capacity for cultural adaptation has been accelerated by the rise of archiving technology. Papyrus scrolls, books and the internet allows us to even more effectively share knowledge with successive generations, opening up an unbridgeable gap between humans and all other animals.”

In your own words, what does the writer mean by “opening up an unbridgeable gap”? [2]

⇒ Definition:

"Opening up": a divide;

"Unbridgeable gap": cannot be crossed.


E.g. 4. “Chinese dams along with engineering projects to make the river navigable by larger vessels, have begun to ravage the river’s ecology by blocking sediment and producing unnatural water flows that dissuade fish migration. Farmers, too, complain that the once predictable floods needed to nourish their paddies have been disrupted by the two existing Chinese dams - and the cavalcade of future hydropower projects will only make things worse.

“Snaking its way from the icy reaches of Tibet to tropical rice paddies near the South China Sea, the Mekong serves as the lifeblood for 70 million people in six different countries. But even as many of the world’s other majestic rivers - the Nile, the Yangtze, the Mississippi - were efficiently exploited for trade or hydropower, the Mekong has until recently largely escaped the imprint of the modern world.”

Explain the meaning of the phrase “efficiently exploited”. [1]

⇒ Definition: to be completely taken advantage of/ the effective overuse of the rivers for the community’s own benefit.


E.g. 5. “Thus, it seems our tribal psychology can extend to groups of seemingly nearly any size. In large countries such as the United Kingdom, Japan, the United States, Brazil, India, and China hundreds of millions and even over a billion people can all be united around a single tribal identity as British or Japanese, American, Indian or Chinese and they will have a tendency to direct their cultural nepotism towards these other members of their now highly extended tribe. If you take this behaviour for granted, just imagine 100,000 dogs or hyenas packed into a sporting arena - not a pretty sight.”

In your own words, explain what the term “cultural nepotism” means. [1]

⇒ Definition: the discrimination or mistreatment of a dominant culture towards someone of a previously different background or the act of prioritising ones of the same cultural background.


E.g. 6. “We also see cooperation as a successful strategy that has propelled our species on a long evolutionary trajectory towards living in larger social groupings that bring people together from different tribal origins. Larger groups can often afford to have armies and to build defensive walls around their settlements. Large groups also benefit from the efficiencies that flow from a division of labour, and from access to a vast shared store of information, skills, technology and good luck.”

Explain what is meant by the word “propelled” in the phrase, “cooperation … has propelled our species … towards living in larger social groupings”. [1]

⇒ Definition: to speed up/ spur/ push/ boost/ accelerate human development towards peacefully coexisting in bigger communities.


E.g. 7. “This dominant model of fashion is untenable. There are much better ways of making a living and representing yourself than through clothes that are environmentally and socially destructive. There is a burgeoning (but not yet fully representative) array of fashion that does not subscribe to this model. Designers such as Bethany Williams embody fashion with integrity. Secondhand, resale and rental are rapidly growing, but rather than replacing at least part of the dominant system, people are still tempted back to these brands, which perpetuate such a warped image of prosperity.”

Explain the meaning of the word “untenable”. [1]

⇒ Definition: indefensible/ cannot be maintained or supported.




📖 Explanation questions

Most explanation questions are 2-mark questions. Thus, the answer should include either 2 points or 2 contrasting ideas. Use your own words to answer explanation questions.

💡 To approach paradox questions, state 2 contrasting points that make the subject a paradox.

💡 To approach irony and oxymoron questions, consider the expectation versus reality.

E.g. 1. “Yet as many Chinese can’t quite fathom the Mekong’s importance to other countries. “This is our part of the river, so we should be able to do what we want with it,” said Hu Tao, a geological engineer. “The other countries can do what they want with their sections of the river.” The Mekong is potent enough to be exploited for electricity. Some of that power ironically, will be exported to countries like Thailand, where hydroelectric projects are blocked by ecologically minded citizens. Nor does the Chinese government feel the need to consult its southern neighbours. Beijing has refused to join the Mekong River Commission which was formed by four other nations. But when the biggest country at the source of the river is not part of the group, it makes the commission basically toothless.”

In your own words, explain what is ironic about China’s export of electricity to Thailand? [2]

⇒ Expectation: support of environmentally friendly initiatives, intended to protect the environment.

Reality: acquiring electricity and power at the expense of the environment, exploiting and damaging the environment.


E.g. 2. “In a society that urges equality, life often resembles a game of favourites, where the “pretty” kids get chosen first, leaving the “ugly” and “fat” kids to watch from the sidelines. This mirrors the concept of “pretty privilege” in society, where people who look a certain way get more opportunities and attention merely because of their appearance. On the other hand, kids who may not be considered conventionally attractive but possess other qualities like kindness, intelligence, or creativity are often ignored or undervalued in a society that places too much emphasis on outward appearances.”

Explain the irony in the bolded line. Use your own words as far as possible. [2]

⇒ Expectation: fair treatment of all peoples

Reality: our world treats more beautiful people better than others

⇒ CONTRAST


E.g. 3. “Shein’s meteoric rise is taking fast fashion, an already resource-depleting model in environmental and social terms, to fresh depths, carving out a new category: ultra-fast fashion. In a week when we have also seen the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change lay out the stark realities of the climate emergency – and with a growing number of people purporting to care deeply about the future of the planet – the success of Shein is somewhat of a paradox.”

Explain why the success of Shein is a paradox. [2]

⇒ On the one hand: Shein has a very bad history of violating human rights by exploiting workers and contributing to environmental damage

On the other hand: remains famous and continues to become more and more desired/ favoured/ wanted

⇒ CONTRAST


E.g. 4. “It is not those on low incomes who drive this industry. The biggest customer base is people with substantial disposable incomes, which raises the question: where did our fashion sense go so wrong?”

Explain the irony present. [2]

⇒ Expectation: people on a budget would support Shein because their clothes are cheap

Reality: people who have a lot of money on hand are patronising Shein

⇒ CONTRAST


E.g. 5. “Singapore’s exclusion of low-wage migrant labour from attaining permanent residency or citizenship sadly does not look set to change. Despite their permanently temporary nature, low-wage migrants often stay for long periods in Singapore. Many who work in Singapore for decades describe strong feelings of belonging and emotional attachment to a nation that has been a home for much of their adult lives.”

Identify the literary device used in the bolded section. [1]

⇒ Oxymoron: permanently temporary nature


Explain how the literary device used is an apt description of the lives of low-wage migrants in Singapore. [2]

⇒ Expectation: the migrants’ stay is temporary and they are not recognised as citizens

Reality: they stay for long periods of time

⇒ CONTRAST





📖 Inference questions

Inference questions have 2 main components.

• Question Focus (QF): key words → literal

• Question Requirement (QR): What does the question want? → figurative

2 mark questions are usually inference questions. Within this class of questions, there are several different subtypes:

💡 To approach tone questions, do not focus on the synonyms during paraphrasing, but rather focus on descriptive words and adjectives.

💡 To approach LFI/ writer's style questions, look at the purpose of the writer or their intended action. Thus, your answer should have a verb present.

E.g. 1. “So, to my mind, there is little doubt that the next century is going to be a bumpy road as money and space become ever more scarce. But if there was ever a species that could tackle these challenges it is our own. It might be surprising, but our genes, in the form of our capacity for culture, have created in us a machine capable of greater cooperation, inventiveness and common good than any other on Earth. And of course it means you can always find a cappuccino just the way you like it no matter where we wake up.”

Why does the writer say that the future is a "bumpy road"? [2]

⇒ Just as it suggests that the path ahead is an uneven one …

... the future of Man is full of various obstacles and challenges as we use up our insufficient resources.

⇒ COMPARISON


What is the writer’s tone in the line, “but if there was ever a species that could tackle these challenges it is our own”? [1]

⇒ Tone: (more positive), hopeful/ determined


Explain why the writer says that our capacity for culture “might be surprising”. [2]

⇒ One would expect man to be self-centred … BUT instead we are able to work together intuitively and creatively devise solutions for larger society.

⇒ SURPRISE: differing expectations and reality


What is implied by the writer through his cappuccino analogy? [2]

⇒ Analogy: comparison between 2 seemingly dissimilar things (based on the similarities)

⇒ By working closely with others from different cultures … we will end up having highly similar/ homogenous tastes and preferences regardless of where we are from.

⇒ what is implied is different from its effect.


E.g. 2. “In the mid-20th century, sociologist Erving Goffman introduced the concept of a “total institution”. This sees inhabitants cut off from wider society under a single authority in an enclosed social space. Sociologists typically study prisons, asylums and other physical spaces as examples. But I believe pet-keeping constitutes a sort of dispersed “total institution”. This is because non-human animals are unnaturally forced under human authority, restrained, and re-socialised. True consent is not possible under such conditions. Animals are groomed to participate and those who are unable to follow the rules of human social life are likely to be punished - sometimes fatally.”

What is the writer’s attitude in the bolded sentences? [1]

⇒ Attitude: convicted, adamant, resolute (unwavering)


E.g. 3. “This is not in any way to suggest that dogs, cats and other species cannot express love and happiness as “pets”. But it is important to recognise that this is entirely manufactured (sometimes quite cruelly) by humans through behaviour “corrections” and the manipulative process of domestication itself. Ultimately, companion animals, by their very position in the social order, are not and cannot be equals. The institution of pet-keeping maintains a social hierarchy which privileges humans and positions all others as objects as lower importance. Their right to existence depends wholly on their potential to benefit humans.”

Describe in a word the writer’s tone in the last sentence, “Their right to existence depends wholly on their potential to benefit humans”. [1]

⇒ cynical/ pragmatic/ pessimistic


E.g. 4. “Snaking its way from the icy reaches of Tibet to tropical rice paddies near the South China Sea, the Mekong serves as the lifeblood for 70 million people in six different countries. But even as many of the world’s other majestic rivers - the Nile, the Yangtze, the Mississippi - were efficiently exploited for trade or hydropower, the Mekong has until recently largely escaped the imprint of the modern world.”

What does the word “lifeblood” suggest about the relationship between the people and the Mekong river? [1]

⇒ The people’s livelihoods depended (impact) heavily (extent) on the Mekong river.


What does the writer’s choice of words in the phrase “efficiently” imply about the writer’s attitude towards the use of the rivers for trade and hydropower? [1]

⇒ Attitude: disapproving of using rivers as simply resources one can drain for a larger benefit


What does the word “imprint” suggest about the impact of development at the expense of nature? [1]

⇒ The impact of development permanently (extent) damages the environment (impact).


E.g. 5. ““Pretty people can get away with anything” “He gets more likes only because he’s good looking. Nothing he says is interesting.” Do you often hear such statements being uttered? They may seem harmless, but they reveal some of society’s more deep-seated biases. One such bias is the “pretty privilege”, wherein people who fit into what society considers pretty enjoy unfair advantages. However, what is interesting is that similar biases exist for those who do not fit societal beauty standards - “ugly privilege” and “fat privilege”, though lurking in the shadows are equally harmful.”

What does “deep-seated” suggest about existing social biases? [1]

⇒ Existing social biases are ingrained (“deep”) into society and are almost impossible to remove or address (“seated”).


Explain what the writer means by the phrase “ugly privilege” and “fat privilege” through “lurking in the shadows”. [2]

⇒ Just as how “lurking in the shadows” means hiding in the darkness in plain sight … despite not being as obvious and not having as much attention drawn to them, prejudices regarding people who do not look as good are still very much prevalent in society.


E.g. 6. “Pretty privilege, often glorified in popular culture, results in preferential treatment across various aspects of life. Media, advertising, and popular culture paint beauty as a coveted crown symbolising success and happiness. Consequently, attractive people are showered in accolades, favoured in work promotions, social interactions and are deemed ideal life partners. Furthermore, “pretty privilege” can also be connected to race, wealth, and gender, worsening existing inequalities. Studies reveal that attractive people from privileged backgrounds often gain even more advantages, while those outside these categories face additional obstacles.”

What does “glorified” suggest about the promotion of beauty in media, advertising and popular culture? [1]

⇒ 1 point question - 1 idea needed only

⇒ Suggests: the promotion of beauty of media makes beauty seem more important than it actually is.

⇒ If the question is 2 marks: the promotion of beauty represents beauty as something admirable and important (impact) in an unjustifiable way (extent).


What does “showered” suggest about the accolades that attractive people get? [1]

⇒ 1 point question - 1 idea needed only

⇒ Suggests: the accolades that attractive people get are in very large numbers.

⇒ If the question is 2 marks: the accolades that attractive people get are in overwhelmingly and extremely large numbers (extent), giving them an advantage/ edge over other people (impact).


Why did the writer cite examples that pretty people are “favoured in work promotions, social interactions and are deemed ideal life partners”? Use your own words as far as possible. [2]

⇒ Examples: support/ substantiate/ prove

⇒ Purpose: to support/ substantiate/ prove that pretty people are prioritised and given so many benefits.


E.g. 7. “Apart from the prices and accessibility, urban farming sees issues in its ‘fields’. Agriculture practices and urban growth have long been considered unhappy bedfellows, with arable land often sacrificed to build apartments and new roads. Similarly, since urban farming tends to be situated in most urban spaces, the perception that the spaces can be used for more economically productive activities has to be challenged alongside concerns regarding the commercial viability of such enterprises. This leads to another consideration involving the management of urban spaces and the optimal re-purposing of existing infrastructure to accommodate the growth of such farms. While farming near customers sounds sensible, to farm at scale efficiently means space is needed due to the large equipment needed for such exploits. Yet, one should not disregard the huge untapped areas in the centres of many cities, often abandoned and forgotten, that we could be using to make the food chain more resilient. One example is the Swiss aquaponics enterprise, Urban Farmers, which took over the derelict rooftop floors of De Schilde, a former Philips TV and phone set factory in The Hague. THrough its converted space, it aims to produce 45 tonnes of vegetables and 19 tonnes of tilapia annually. We need to envision the opportunities in such unusual urban spaces and invest in their development to see the growth of such farms.”

Why does the author use inverted commas in the bolded part? [1]

⇒ Inverted commas: not taken in a literal sense - have a hidden meaning (not always sarcastic)

⇒ Urban farming does not require actual fields in the literal sense since it is carried out in urban spaces.


Explain what the author means by “unhappy bedfellows”. [2]

⇒ Just as how “unhappy bedfellows” are people who are forced by circumstances to share a bed but do not like each other …

… agricultural practices and urban growth share a close relationship but the relationship does not go hand in hand.

⇒ COMPARISON


Why does the author use the word “yet”? [1]

⇒ “yet” denotes contrast

⇒ Emphasises opportunities offered by urban farming despite its space requirements (to accommodate huge equipment to grow on a large scale)

⇒ [2]: Urban farming suggests the need for a substantial amount of space to accommodate huge equipment to grow on a large scale. While this might seem impossible, the word “yet” emphasises how cities can utilise abandoned and forgotten areas.

⇒ CONTRAST


E.g. 8. “Humans are becoming ever more urban in their habits, yet it’s only recently that scientists have attempted to quantify the impact of urbanisation on levels of biodiversity around the world. One striking recent global analysis of bird and plant diversity showed just how dramatically the built environment hits birds. For every species spotted in a given urban environment, there are typically more than 10 in an equivalent area of non-urban habitat.

“This might sound like a dismal statistic, but it’s also an opportunity - a valuable benchmark from which we can now improve. “There are many reasons why urban biodiversity matters,” says Mark Goddard, a biologist at the University of Leeds and one of the authors on the recent paper.”

Explain the writer’s choice of the word “dismal” to describe the statistic in the last sentence of the previous paragraph. [1]

⇒ Tone: disheartening/ disappointing OR discouraging/ worrying/ glum/ hopeless/ bleak


Why is the statistic also a “valuable benchmark”? [1]

⇒ The statistic can help Man make positive changes/ improve the environment (infer from “valuable”) by assessing effectiveness of efforts to conserve the environment (“benchmark”).


E.g. 9. “In short, there are still plenty of city-dwelling species to protect. But how can we make our cities more inviting places for these and other creatures - and, to put it bluntly, why should we bother?”

What does the question “Why should we bother?” imply about people’s attitude of the protection of city-dwelling species? [1]

⇒ Attitude: indifferent/ nonchalant


E.g. 10. “Few cities have been tangling with urban ecology for as long as Berlin. Slow economic recovery after the second world war meant that reconstruction took a long time. This provided local ecologists with the ideal conditions to develop an ambitious ecological research programme in the bombed-out wastelands of West Berlin, says Jens Lachmund, a sociologist at the University of Maastricht in the Netherlands. The pioneering work at Berlin had several consequences, notably the formation of significant natural spaces within the city such as the Südgelände Nature Park. “Berlin has indeed benefited a lot from being a case study in urban ecology,” says Lachmund. The proximity of a city to a national park or other protected area is also likely to make it more species rich. With the Nairobi National Park just a few miles from the city centre, Kenya’s capital boasts more than 300 species of bird.”

What does the phrase “tangling with” imply about Berlin’s attempt to create a functioning urban ecosystem? [2]

⇒ It has been a struggle (impact) for Berlin to strike a balance between urbanisation and the protection of city-dwelling species as it is a complicated (extent) issue.

OR

Just as a string dangles precariously ...

… the phrase suggests that it is a challenge to strike a balance between urbanisation and conservation.


E.g. 11. “Thankfully, ground sentiment about low-wage migrants is fast changing. Thanks to the work of non-government organisations (NGOs), the welfare of migrant workers is catered to and there is ample coverage of their lives in the local media. As a result, there is now far more sympathy for these men and women than there was even a decade ago. There is greater acknowledgement of the important work that they do in building and maintaining the material fabric of our city and in taking care of our children and senior citizens.”

What is the writer’s tone at the start of the paragraph? [1]

⇒ Tone: approving/ relieved/ optimistic


What does the writer imply with the phrase “ground sentiment”? [2]

⇒ Inference: A viewpoint (sentiment) felt by Singaporean citizens, not the leaders (ground)

• Metaphors, similies, analogies: to compare

E.g. 12. “The Mekong is not so unyielding these days. In 2001, Chinese crews, brought in by Southeast Asian governments eager to increase traffic and trade, began blasting and dredging a stretch of the river running from Burma to Thailand. Clearing away islands, reefs and rapids that once blocked the passage of ships. “Chinese are natural businessman,” says Liu Jingchun, a Chinese boat captain who transports goods between Yunnan and northern Thailand. “For so many years, we shut ourselves off from doing business. Now that we’re allowed to trade again, it’s like a giant floodgate has opened.”

Explain the meaning of the metaphor “floodgate”. [2]

⇒ Metaphor: comparison

⇒ Just as an open floodgate is a gate which when opened, water outpours …

… China’s break from isolation has resulted in the burgeoning of trade and business.

⇒ COMPARISON


E.g. 13. “Among primates, it’s baboons that do best in a human-altered world. One of the best-studied urban populations is in Cape Town, South Africa, where the chacma baboon has been living cheek by jowl with humans since at least the 15th century. In spite of the increasing pressures on the wild spaces around the city, the local baboon population is estimated to have risen from 365 in 1998 to 475 in 2011. Part of the baboon’s success is their strong social network, their unfussy diet and behavioural adaptability. In urban settings like Cape Town, baboons appear to have honed their raiding skills, zipping into densely populated areas to steal high-value human foodstuffs. Similarly, the Barbary macaques of Gibraltar, emboldened by their friendly contact with tourists, have been carrying out forays further into human territory, raiding dustbind and running amok.”

What does the idiom “cheek by jowl” suggest about the way baboons and humans live together? [2]

⇒ Idiom "cheek by jowl": close together

⇒ The baboons and humans live side by side (together) in close physical proximity (close) to one another.

• Repetition: to emphasise

E.g. 14. “Stroll into your local Starbucks and you will find yourself part of a cultural experiment on a scale never seen before on this planet. In less than half a century, the coffee chain has grown from a single outlet in Seattle to nearly 20,000 shops in around 60 countries. Each year, its near identical stores serve cups of near identical coffee in near identical cups to hundreds of thousands of people. For the first time in history, your morning cappuccino is the same no matter whether you are sipping it in Tokyo, New York, Bangkok or Buenos Aires.”

Why does the writer repeat the lines "near identical"? [1]

⇒ The repetition emphasises the similarity of human culture, despite being from all around the world.

• Rhetorical questions: make the reader ponder/ wonder/ consider why

E.g. 15. “Of course, it is not just Starbucks. Select any global brand from Coca Cola to Facebook and the chances are you will see or feel their presence in most countries around the world. It is easy to see this homogenisation in terms of loss of diversity, identity or the westernisation of society. But the rapid pace of change also raises the more interesting question of humans and their many distinct cultures in the first place. And, if diversity is a part of our psychological make-up, how will we fare in a world that is increasingly bringing together people from different cultural backgrounds and traditions?”

What is the technique used by the writer in the line “ … how will we fare in a world that is increasingly bringing together people from different cultural backgrounds and traditions?” [1]

⇒ Rhetorical question


What is the effect of the technique utilised? [1]

⇒ Effect: provokes the reader to make them consider the impacts of a diverse world.


E.g. 16. “According to the United Kingdom veterinary charity The People’s Dispensary for Sick Animals, half of Britons own a pet. Many of these owners view their pets as family members. But although we love them, care for them, celebrate their birthdays and mourn them when they pass, is it ethical to keep pets in the first place? Some animal rights activists, myself included, would argue that it is not.”

Why does the writer use a rhetorical question? [2]

⇒ Purpose: the writer wants the readers to think, to ponder and wonder whether keeping pets is humane and justified.


What is the writer implying by using the phrase “myself included”? [2]

⇒ The writer wishes to declare her allegiance to the beliefs of the animal rights activists and ethicists who are against keeping pets.


E.g. 17. “It is not those on low incomes who drive this industry. The biggest customer base is people with substantial disposable incomes, which raises the question: where did our fashion sense go so wrong?”

Why does the writer use a rhetorical question? [2]

⇒ Purpose: wants the reader to ponder/ consider/ wonder deeply how people with a lot of spare cash/savings on hand are contributing to the problem of consumerism and all the environmental repercussions that follow (inference).

• Inverted commas: to denote sarcasm or a different hidden meaning

E.g. 18. “Through this forced dependency and domestication, the lives of companion animals are almost completely controlled by humans. They can be terminated at any time for the most trivial of reasons - including behavioural ‘problems’, for belonging to a stereotyped breed, or the owner’s inability (or unwillingness) to pay for veterinary treatment.”

Why does the writer place “problems” in inverted commas? [2]

⇒ Purpose: the writer wants the readers to think, to ponder and wonder whether keeping pets is humane and justified.


What is the writer implying by using the phrase “myself included”? [2]

⇒ Inverted commas: to denote sarcasm

⇒ The “problems” are not actually problems, but are simply imagined/ perceived by humans.

• Examples: to support/ substantiate/ prove/ (illustrate)

E.g. 19. “There is a growing global trend to consider pets as part of the family. In fact, millions of people around the world love their pets, enjoying their companionship, going for walks, playing and even talking to them. And there is evidence suggesting that attachment to pets is good for human health and even helps build community.”

How does the writer illustrate that people “consider pets as part of the family”? [2]

⇒ The writer illustrates through examples showing how pet owners show love and affection for pets as they would with a human family member.


E.g. 20. “As pets play an increasingly important role in our lives, societies create laws and institutions to protect companion animals from cruelty and neglect. In most jurisdictions, regulation of shelters and pounds has not evolved to reflect the beloved status of many pets, and instead consider pets as property. If a lost pet is not reunited with an owner within a few days it can be sold to a new family, to a research lab, or be euthanised. However, some countries, such as India and Italy, have legislated against the euthanasia of healthy shelter animals.”

Why does the writer cite India and Italy as examples? [1]

⇒ The writer wishes to prove how there are exceptional cases of nations and organisations that advocate for the protection of animals from mistreatment.

• Statistics: to illustrate

E.g. 21. “The strength of the human-animal bond has resulted in the creation of not-for-profit animal rescues whose mission is to “pull” lost and abandoned animals from shelters before they are euthanised or sold for research. For example, Underdog Railroad in Toronto, Ontario, rescues dogs and cats from high-kill shelters as well as those offered “free to a good home” online. In addition, animal rescue organisations like Elderdog provide older adults with help to care for their pets, and The Humane Society International - Canada assists in sterilisation programmes as well as advocating for and rescuing animals in the international dog and cat meat industries. They closed three South Korean dog meat farms and two slaughterhouses in 2018, rescuing 512 dogs, many of whom found homes in Canada and the USA.”

Why does the writer cite statistics? [2]

⇒ Statistics: to illustrate the positive and significant impact that these organisations have to help animals

• "What does (word) suggest" question: usually 2 marks. This type of question covers the extent and impact

E.g. 22. “This is not to say that cooperation is easy, or that it is never subject to reversals. Just look at the outpouring of cultural diversity that sprang up with the collapse of the Soviet Union. Despite being suppressed for decades, almost overnight smaller states like Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan reappeared, all differentiated by culture, ethnicity, and language.”

What do the examples of Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan suggest about cultural diversity? [2]

⇒ The examples prove that cultural diversity is inherent/ intrinsic/ innate/ natural (extent) and it cannot be quelled/ subdued/ contained/ eliminated/ wiped out (impact).


E.g. 23. “Yet as many Chinese can’t quite fathom the Mekong’s importance to other countries. “This is our part of the river, so we should be able to do what we want with it,” said Hu Tao, a geological engineer. “The other countries can do what they want with their sections of the river.” The Mekong is potent enough to be exploited for electricity. Some of that power ironically, will be exported to countries like Thailand, where hydroelectric projects are blocked by ecologically minded citizens. Nor does the Chinese government feel the need to consult its southern neighbours. Beijing has refused to join the Mekong River Commission which was formed by four other nations. But when the biggest country at the source of the river is not part of the group, it makes the commission basically toothless.”

Explain why the Mekong River Commission is “toothless” without China’s participation. [2]
